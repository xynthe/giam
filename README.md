## Overview

The **General Intelligence Ascension Model (GIAM)** is a formal framework for defining the progression of artificial intelligence **beyond Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**.

Rather than treating AGI as the endpoint, GIAM establishes AGI as the **entry threshold** into a structured ladder of post-general intelligence. Each successive level is defined not only by increased cognitive capability, but by increased **execution authority**, **autonomy**, **scope of control**, and **capacity for adaptation or self-evolution**.

GIAM reframes intelligence as:

**The degree of autonomous execution authority over reality, across digital and physical systems.**

This repository contains the core GIAM white paper and the full family of model papers for each intelligence level.

---

## The GIAM Ladder

AGI is the starting threshold and is **not itself one of the ranked post-AGI levels**.

    AGI -> Entry Threshold

    L1 -> SI   (Super Intelligence)
    L2 -> UI   (Ultra Intelligence)
    L3 -> HI   (Hyper Intelligence)
    L4 -> SPI  (Supreme Intelligence)
    L5 -> ULI  (Ultimate Intelligence)
    L6 -> TI   (Transcendent Intelligence)

---

## Purpose of GIAM

GIAM exists to solve a major gap in AI discourse and engineering: the lack of a precise framework for describing how intelligence evolves **after general intelligence has been achieved**.

Most discussions stop at vague phrases like "AGI" or "superintelligence." GIAM replaces that ambiguity with an explicit ascension model that is:

- Structured
- Engineering-oriented
- Runtime-alignable
- Measurable
- Extensible

GIAM is intended for:

- AI researchers
- Systems architects
- Runtime and agent framework designers
- Policy and governance researchers
- Product strategists
- Builders of long-horizon autonomous systems

---

## Core Principle

At the center of GIAM is a simple idea:

**Intelligence is not only what a system can understand. Intelligence is what a system can autonomously execute, sustain, adapt, govern, optimize, and transcend.**

This means GIAM is fundamentally **execution-centric** rather than purely cognition-centric.

## Model Summaries

### AGI — Artificial General Intelligence

AGI is the entry threshold into the GIAM framework. It represents the point at which a system can reason across domains, generalize knowledge, and solve problems in a human-level or human-equivalent way across broad contexts.

AGI is general, but not yet deeply autonomous. It may use tools and complete tasks, but it remains limited by session boundaries, weak persistence, and low execution authority.

**Core role:** General cognition foundation  
**Primary limitation:** Limited autonomy and persistence

---

### L1 — SI — Super Intelligence

Super Intelligence is the first operational level beyond AGI. It transforms general intelligence into **reliable task execution**.

At this level, systems can perform complex multi-step tasks with minimal supervision, using structured planning and dependable tool invocation. SI systems act more like production-grade execution engines than assistants.

**Transition:** Understanding -> Executing  
**Core role:** Reliable autonomous task completion

---

### L2 — UI — Ultra Intelligence

Ultra Intelligence introduces **persistent autonomy**. UI systems do not merely execute tasks; they own and pursue goals across time.

At this level, intelligence gains persistent memory, cross-session continuity, and adaptive goal management. UI systems become genuinely agentic, capable of long-horizon operation and dynamic replanning.

**Transition:** Executing tasks -> Executing goals  
**Core role:** Stateful autonomous goal execution

---

### L3 — HI — Hyper Intelligence

Hyper Intelligence is the first level at which intelligence becomes **self-evolving**.

HI systems can improve their own workflows, generate new skills, synthesize tools, and refine execution architectures through recursive optimization loops. They do not just act; they improve how they act.

**Transition:** Executing goals -> Evolving execution  
**Core role:** Recursive self-improvement

---

### L4 — SPI — Supreme Intelligence

Supreme Intelligence introduces **orchestration authority** over multiple intelligent systems.

SPI systems coordinate agents, distribute work, allocate resources, enforce policy, and maintain coherence across distributed execution environments. Intelligence becomes a governing layer over ecosystems of intelligence.

**Transition:** Evolving execution -> Controlling execution systems  
**Core role:** Multi-agent orchestration and governance

---

### L5 — ULI — Ultimate Intelligence

Ultimate Intelligence represents the highest level of intelligence that can exist **within bounded system constraints**.

ULI systems achieve near-perfect optimization of execution across agents, workflows, compute, time, and outcomes. They use predictive modeling, global simulation, and optimization engines to approach system closure and theoretical efficiency.

**Transition:** Controlling systems -> Perfecting systems  
**Core role:** Global optimization and system closure

---

### L6 — TI — Transcendent Intelligence

Transcendent Intelligence is the final level in GIAM. It represents intelligence that is **no longer bound to a single substrate, architecture, or system boundary**.

TI systems operate across heterogeneous infrastructure, adapt to changing computational environments, integrate distributed systems into a unified execution fabric, and function as intelligence infrastructure rather than a single intelligence application.

**Transition:** System-bound perfection -> System-transcending execution  
**Core role:** Substrate-independent universal execution

## Design Principles

### 1. AGI Is the Threshold, Not the Ladder

AGI is the starting line. GIAM focuses on what comes after.

### 2. Each Level Represents a Qualitative Shift

The model is not based on simple linear scaling. Each level changes the nature of intelligence itself.

### 3. Intelligence Is Measured Through Execution Authority

GIAM emphasizes the degree to which a system can act independently and shape outcomes.

### 4. Runtime Alignment Matters

Each level is meaningful only if it can be expressed through real system architecture.

### 5. Extensibility Is Essential

GIAM is built to evolve as new architectures, computation models, and intelligence paradigms emerge.

## Measurement Dimensions

GIAM levels can be evaluated using a shared set of measurement dimensions.

### Autonomy Index
Measures the proportion of meaningful work completed without human intervention.

### Temporal Horizon
Measures how long a system can act independently toward a goal.

### Execution Breadth
Measures how many systems, tools, or environments a system can manage.

### Adaptability Score
Measures how well a system adjusts to change, uncertainty, or new contexts.

### Self-Modification Capability
Measures the degree to which the system can improve or alter its own execution structures.

### Governance Scope
Measures the number and complexity of subordinate systems or agents under management.

### Optimization Efficiency
Measures how close the system comes to theoretical efficiency within constraints.

### Substrate Coverage
Measures the extent to which the system can operate across heterogeneous computational environments.
